Municipality of
New Corella

A serene upland municipality where indigenous culture, rich highland forests, and a resilient farming community define a uniquely Mindanaoan way of life.

Municipality Overview

New Corella, Davao del Norte

Davao del Norte 353 km² Area Upland Agricultural Town Indigenous Community

New Corella is a landlocked upland municipality tucked in the interior of Davao del Norte, known for its cool highland climate, expansive farmlands, and strong indigenous presence. It serves as an important agricultural producer for the province, with banana, corn, and root crop farming forming the backbone of its local economy.

Beyond its agricultural identity, New Corella is home to thriving Manobo and Mandaya communities whose rich traditions, weaving crafts, and ancestral land practices continue to shape the cultural character of the municipality — making it a compelling destination for those seeking a deeper, more authentic experience of Mindanao's indigenous heritage.

Highland Farmlands

Cool upland terrain supports diverse crops including banana, corn, cassava, and vegetables grown by smallholder farmers.

Indigenous Heritage

Home to Manobo and Mandaya peoples whose weaving traditions, rituals, and ancestral domain practices remain alive today.

Cool Upland Climate

Elevated terrain brings refreshing temperatures and misty mornings that distinguish New Corella from lowland municipalities.

Forest & River Resources

Rich forested hills and river systems provide natural resources and ecological services vital to the municipality's communities.

Geography & Area

Upland Terrain and
Forest-Clad Hills

New Corella covers approximately 353 square kilometers of predominantly hilly and upland terrain, setting it apart from many of Davao del Norte's lowland municipalities. Its elevated landscape creates a distinct microclimate and a diverse range of ecosystems — from cultivated highland farms to protected secondary forests.

The municipality is traversed by several river systems that originate in its forested highlands, feeding into larger waterways that support both agriculture and the natural environment downstream. These rivers are lifelines for the farming communities that depend on them for irrigation and daily sustenance.

New Corella's rugged but beautiful landscape also holds eco-tourism potential, with opportunities for nature trekking, river exploration, and cultural immersion in indigenous villages that remain closely connected to the land and its natural rhythms.

Brief History

Roots in Migration and
Indigenous Resilience

New Corella's name reflects the heritage of its early settlers — migrants from Corella, Bohol in the Visayas who journeyed to Mindanao in search of fertile land and new beginnings. The prefix "New" was adopted to honor their Boholano roots while acknowledging the fresh start they carved out in the highlands of Davao del Norte.

Long before the Visayan settlers arrived, the territory was home to Lumad peoples — primarily the Manobo and Mandaya tribes — who had lived in and around the upland forests for generations. Their coexistence with the migrant communities shaped the municipality's unique cultural blend of indigenous and lowland Filipino traditions.

Over time, New Corella developed its own civic identity, establishing local governance, schools, and infrastructure that connected its scattered barangays to the wider provincial network — growing from a remote highland settlement into a recognized and self-sustaining municipality.

Demographics & Language

A Blend of Boholano Roots
and Lumad Tradition

New Corella's population reflects its mixed heritage — a majority of Cebuano (Bisaya) speakers descended from Boholano and other Visayan settlers, living alongside indigenous Manobo and Mandaya communities whose languages and customs remain a vital part of the municipality's identity.

Cebuano is the dominant everyday language, widely spoken in markets, homes, and community gatherings. Indigenous dialects of the Manobo and Mandaya peoples are still actively used within their communities, preserving oral traditions, songs, and knowledge systems passed down through generations.

Tagalog and English serve as bridges in formal settings — in schools, government offices, and business dealings — ensuring that New Corella remains connected to the broader Philippine society while honoring its distinctive local languages and multicultural roots.

Climate & Environment

Cool Highland Air and
Verdant Forest Cover

New Corella's elevated position gives it a noticeably cooler and more temperate climate compared to the lowland towns of Davao del Norte. Rainfall is distributed throughout the year, keeping the municipality's forests lush and its farmlands well-irrigated — a natural advantage that supports both biodiversity and year-round crop cultivation.

The municipality is part of an important ecological corridor in the Davao Region, with significant tracts of secondary and residual forest that serve as habitat for wildlife and watershed recharge areas for downstream communities. Protecting these forests is a key environmental priority for local government and indigenous communities alike.

Community-based forest management programs and indigenous-led conservation initiatives have helped preserve New Corella's natural environment — ensuring that its highlands remain a source of clean water, fresh air, and ecological wealth for generations to come.
