Waterfalls of
Davao del Norte

Plunging cascades, hidden forest pools, and the thunderous roar of highland rivers — the waterfalls of Davao del Norte are nature's most breathtaking spectacle.

Tourism Overview

Cascading Wonders Across the Province

Multiple Falls Sites Forest Settings Trekking Access Photography Spots

Davao del Norte's abundant rainfall and dramatic highland terrain combine to create some of Mindanao's most spectacular waterfalls — natural wonders tucked within forest ecosystems, cascading over ancient rock formations, and feeding the river systems that sustain the province's agricultural heartland below. From easily accessible falls near populated barangays to remote cascades deep in the mountains of Talaingod, the province offers waterfall experiences for every level of adventurer.

These cascading gems are more than just scenic attractions — they are the visible expression of Davao del Norte's extraordinary natural wealth, fed by the year-round rainfall and intact highland forests that make the province one of the most ecologically vibrant in the entire Philippines. Visiting them is an invitation to step into the living landscape of Mindanao.

Featured Waterfall

Hagimit Falls:
Samal's Jungle Cascade

Nestled in the forested interior of the Island Garden City of Samal, Hagimit Falls is one of the most beloved natural attractions in Davao del Norte — a series of cascading waterfalls and natural rock pools surrounded by lush tropical vegetation that provides a cool and refreshing escape from the island's sunny beaches. The falls drop in multiple tiers over mossy boulders, creating pools of clear, cold water perfect for a refreshing swim.

Reaching Hagimit Falls involves a short but rewarding trek through the island's secondary forest — a trail lined with ferns, flowering plants, and the sounds of birds and insects that give the experience an immersive quality rarely found in more commercial waterfall destinations. The journey is as enjoyable as the destination itself, particularly for families and nature lovers looking for a gentle outdoor adventure.

Hagimit Falls is accessible by tricycle or habal-habal from Samal's main port, making it an ideal addition to any island itinerary. A small entrance fee supports the maintenance of the site, and basic picnic facilities are available for those who want to spend a full day enjoying the falls and the surrounding forest environment.

Remote Highland Falls

Mountain Cascades of
Talaingod and Kapalong

For the more adventurous traveler, the highlands of Talaingod and Kapalong conceal a series of spectacular waterfalls that plunge from forested cliffs into deep, cold pools far below. These remote cascades are accessible only by multi-hour treks through mountain forest — journeys that pass through the ancestral domain of the Ata-Manobo and Mandaya peoples and offer some of the most dramatic highland scenery in the entire Davao Region.

The waterfalls of Talaingod are fed by the same mountain rainfall that sustains the old-growth forests above them — their flow is powerful and consistent year-round, with the volume increasing dramatically during the wetter months when the highland rivers swell with rainfall collected across hundreds of square kilometers of forested watershed.

Access to these remote falls requires coordination with local indigenous community guides, who provide safe navigation through often unmarked forest trails, cultural context for the landscapes being traversed, and assurance that the visit is conducted in a way that respects the environmental and cultural protocols of the ancestral domain communities whose land these waterfalls flow through.

Swimming & Recreation

Cool Mountain Waters
and Natural Pools

One of the greatest pleasures of waterfall tourism in Davao del Norte is the opportunity to swim in the natural pools formed at the base of the falls — cold, crystal-clear water fed directly from highland rivers and filtered through layers of rock and forest soil. These natural swimming holes offer a refreshing and deeply satisfying experience that no resort pool can replicate.

The pools beneath falls like Hagimit on Samal Island are shallow enough for families with children to enjoy safely, while the deeper pools beneath the highland cascades of Kapalong attract experienced swimmers and cliff-jumping enthusiasts looking for a more thrilling aquatic experience surrounded by unspoiled nature.

Waterfall picnicking is a beloved local tradition in Davao del Norte — families and groups of friends bring food and spend entire days at waterfall sites, enjoying the cool mist, the sound of rushing water, and the natural beauty of settings that serve as the perfect backdrop for a full day of relaxation, swimming, and communal enjoyment of the province's extraordinary natural gifts.

Travel Tips & Best Practices

Planning Your
Waterfall Adventure

The best time to visit most waterfalls in Davao del Norte is during the drier months of November to April, when trails are more manageable, river crossings are safer, and water clarity is at its best. However, waterfalls are most powerful and dramatic during or just after rainfall — a trade-off worth considering depending on your preferred experience.

For accessible falls like Hagimit on Samal, bring water shoes or sandals with grip, a change of clothes, sunscreen, and a waterproof bag for valuables. Trails to remote highland falls require more serious preparation — sturdy trekking boots, rain gear, sufficient food and water, and a local guide who knows the terrain.

Always practice Leave No Trace principles at waterfall sites — carry out all rubbish, avoid using soap or shampoo in natural pools, and keep noise levels respectful of both the natural environment and other visitors. Many of Davao del Norte's most beautiful waterfalls remain pristine because the communities that live nearby take great pride in protecting them — joining that effort is the mark of a responsible and welcome visitor.
